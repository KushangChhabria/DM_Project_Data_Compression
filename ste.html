Shanon's Theory of Entropy



The Shannon entropy is a measure of uncertainty in a random process. In the case of rolling element machinery, a system without any failures tends to produce a more random signal, while a machine experiencing failures often exhibits a more deterministic signal. Consequently, the Shannon entropy will vary between the two scenarios.

Shannon's theory introduces the concept of a data communication system consisting of three fundamental components: a data source, a communication channel, and a receiver.

Given the probabilities of the letters in the alphabet χ = {α1, α2, ...} as p(x), the average number of bits per symbol required by any lossless compression strategy is always greater than or equal to the entropy H(X).

Therefore, the entropy H(X) serves as a lower bound for achievable compression.

In the context of data compression methods, the proximity of the average number of bits per symbol to the entropy is used to measure their effectiveness. The goal is to minimize the gap between the two measures, indicating a more efficient compression strategy.
